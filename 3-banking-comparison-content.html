ü§î Wait ‚Äî Is This Any Different?

Not really! Circle's business model is exactly like a bank's ‚Äî hold deposits, earn yield, keep the spread. The difference? Circle's reserves are more transparent (published monthly) and more conservative (T-Bills only, not loans). Some newer stablecoins are starting to share yield with holders.

üí° The Strategic Insight

The expensive part isn't moving crypto ‚Äî it's bridging between old and new systems. Companies that can stay in stablecoins (pay suppliers, receive payments, hold treasury) avoid the toll booths entirely. This is why crypto-native businesses have a cost advantage.

üîë Three Things That Change Everything
üîÑ
Portability
Traditional: Switching banks = paperwork, direct deposit changes, weeks of hassle. Your account is tied to that institution.
Crypto: Import 12 words into any wallet, instantly. Your money isn't "in" Coinbase ‚Äî it's on the blockchain. The wallet is just a window.
üîç
Reserve Transparency
Traditional: Fractional reserve banking. Your bank has ~10% of deposits on hand. The rest is loaned out. You trust their risk management.
Crypto: Circle publishes monthly attestations. 100% reserves in cash and T-Bills. Auditable on-chain. Still trust Circle, but more transparent.
‚öîÔ∏è
Real Competition
Traditional: SWIFT has no real competitor. Correspondent banking is entrenched. Fees stay high because there's no alternative.
Crypto: If Ethereum fees spike, people move to Solana. Networks compete in real-time on speed and cost. Innovation is constant.

üéØ The Bottom Line

Stablecoins aren't replacing banks ‚Äî they're unbundling them. The functions that banks combine (custody, payments, lending, exchange) are being separated into specialized, competing layers.

The money moves faster and cheaper. The tradeoff is you become responsible for security and there's no one to call when things go wrong.

For banking professionals, the question isn't whether this technology matters ‚Äî it's which parts of your value chain are most vulnerable to competition from systems that settle in seconds for fractions of a cent.
